Collection: Orson Welles Great Mysteries
========================================

:: Title
Orson Welles Great Mysteries

:: Date
1973-1974

:: Description
Orson Welles Great Mysteries is a British television series originally transmitted between 1973 and 1974, produced by Anglia Television for the ITV network. The series is an anthology of mystery stories. Each episode is introduced by Orson Welles, the only regular actor in the series, whose appearances were confined to the introductory and closing sequences.

:: References
https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orson_Welles_Great_Mysteries

a grubby stranger, who knows the innkeeper is secretly the notorious pirate

:: Title
The Leather Funnel

:: Date
1973-09-08

:: Description
A young man talks with the uncle of the girl he wants to marry, and is told a
strange story about a curious funnel made of leather. While falling asleep next
to the device, he has a presentiment that it was once used as an instrument of
torture.

Directed by: Alan Gibson. Story by: David Ambrose, based on the story by Arthur
Conan Doyle.

:: Ratings
4 <paul>
5 <mikael>

:: Choice Themes
the supernatural [At the heart of the story there were two distinct supernatural concepts that were explicitly contraposed with scientific thinking: The idea that strong memories could be imprinted on nearby objects and later be re-experienced by someone dreaming nearby; and the idea that an evildoer, perhaps by appealing to Satan, could be reincarnated in order to finish their wicked purpose.]

:: Major Themes
crackpot theory [Arnaud posited that physical objects maintain records of key events associated with them that can be accessed through dreams. In this manner, the titular leather funnel was revealed to be a torture device of unspecified function.]
dream interpretation [] {For discussion}
uncle and niece [The story turns on Arnaud trying to alert Stephen that Arnaud's niece, Veronique, who was Stephen's love interest, was a family annihilator in the making.]
murder [Marie Madelaine D'Aubray was condemned to death after being found guilty of devil worship and the murders of her father and two of her three brothers. By leaving the third brother alive, Marie had failed in her bid end the line of D'Aubray family. She vowed to return after death to finish the job, and Veronique may have been her second coming.]
romantic love [Stephen was besotted Veronique and intended to propose to her. He changed his mind after learning, by supernatural means, that she was a reincarnated parricide destined to end her former father's line. Stephen evidently didn't care for the marriage prospects thence implied, for he departed the scene in terror.]
torture [A mystery left deliberately unresolved was that of what precise use the leather funnel had had in Marie's torture. Arnaud, perhaps breaking the fourth wall, commented disdainfully of such personallity types that would feel urged to know the specifics of inventive torture methods.]
greedy heir [Veronique alleged tat her uncle was making up fibs in order to ruin her love prospects because he would lose control of her inheritance the day she married.]

:: Minor Themes
what it is like in a legal proceeding [At an Inquisition-like trial, Marie Madelaine D'Aubray was condemned to death after being found guilty of devil worship and the murders of her father and two of her three brothers.]
capital punishment [Marie Madelaine D'Aubray was condemned to death after being found guilty of of devil worship and the murders of her father and two of her three brothers.]
devil worship [Marie Madelaine D'Aubray was convicted of devil worship, a charge which she did not deny.]
fear [Marie Madelaine D'Aubray confessed to her executioner that she was afraid, not of him but rather of the physical pain that she was about to be put through.]
coping with being tortured [Marie Madelaine D'Aubray lost her nerve after the executioner walked her into the torture chamber and she took sight of the instruments with which she was about to be tortured.]

:: Title
A Terribly Strange Bed

:: Date
1973-09-15

:: Description
Charles Faulkner, a young American gambler in Paris, should have left the
casino after striking it lucky. Why the devil did he agree to sleep in the
casino's guest bed? This was to be the most horrible night he had ever lived.

Directed by: Alan Cooke. Story by: Anthony Fowles, based on the story by Wilkie
Collins.

:: Ratings
4 <paul>
5 <mikael>

:: Choice Themes
what if I were in a tight spot [Charles made an ill-advised decision to spend the night in the guest bedroom of a seedy gambling house after winning a sizable sum of money at the roulette wheel. He was soon distressed to find that he was locked inside the room. After turning in for the night, he was nearly crushed like a grape in his sleep by the titular bed, which his captors operated as a kind of press, before making a harrowing escape out of the window.]

:: Major Themes
friendship [Charles and George were fast friends. George walked out of the seedy gambling house on Charles because he feared for his safety after Charles won a sizable sum at the roulette wheel. The story concluded with George returning to the scene where he saved Charles from explaining his way out of a tricky situation.]
gambling [The story is premised on a young American gambler, named Charles, winning big at the roulette wheel in a seedy Paris gambling house.]
what if I suddenly got some free money [Charles won a sizable sum at the roulette wheel in a seedy Paris gambling house. The amount of money was sufficiently large that he attracted the attention of some unsavory characters who plotted to murder him in the middle of the night and take the money for themselves.]
greed for riches [Lemerle hatched a devious plot to crush Charles like a grape in his sleep and steal the sizable sum that he'd won at the roulette wheel.]
life in late modern France [The story is set in post-Napoleonic restoration Paris and makes a point about showcasing what nightlife in that city might have been like.]
inebriated interaction [Charles simultanously drunk himself silly and won a huge amount of money. A point of the story, nearly spelled out by Lemerle?, is the ease with which a drunk can be parted from his posessions.]

:: Minor Themes
law enforcement [It seemed that Charles would have a lot of explaining to do when a police officer found him lying on the sidewalk in the middle of the night with a bag full of coins. However, George came out of nowhere to vouch for his fast friend Charles, thereby allaying the officer's suspicions.]
honor [The soldier Lemerle? feigned outrage at being offered charity for helping Charles recover some coins from a putitive thief. Once, he said, that offer would have made him insist upon a duel!]
reliable streak of luck [Charles refused to depart from the roulette table because he was on an implausible winning streak. Fate proved him right, in the story.]

:: Title
Trial for Murder

:: Date
1974-02-17

:: Description
In the 19th century, Charles Stubbs is a perfectly ordinary citizen, until one
day he sees the terrifying spirit of a murdered old man appear to him. Shortly
thereafter, he is summoned to be head of the jury on a murder trial, where a
man called Higgins is accused of murdering an old man after he was caught
cheating with his wife. Stubbs does not want to attend, but when the ghost
appears to him again, he decides to accept the task. At the trial, Higgins
complains about Stubbs being on the jury, despite the two never having met -
since it is revealed that the apparition of Stubbs holding a noose appeared to
him in jail every night. During the trial, both Stubbs and the defense lawyer,
as well as a prostitute trying to provide an alibi for Higgins, keep seeing
glimpses of the ghost, and eventually even the last opposing jury member is
convinced of Higgins' guilt, and he is found guilty. Later, when Stubbs reads
in the papers about Higgins' execution, the ghost appears to him a final time,
nodding thanks, then fading away, having been put to rest.

Directed by: Peter Sykes. Story by: David Lawrie, based on the short story by
C.A Collins and Charles Dickens.
